John Adams by David McClullough | Book Review | Matt Bramer

August 16, 2024

David McCullough's "John Adams" is a masterful biography that brings to life one of America's most underappreciated Founding Fathers. Published in 2001, this Pulitzer Prize-winning work offers readers a comprehensive and engaging portrait of the nation's second president, shedding light on his pivotal role in shaping the United States during its formative years.

McCullough, known for his ability to breathe life into historical figures, presents John Adams as a man of principle, intellect, and unwavering patriotism. The biography spans Adams' life from his humble beginnings in Braintree (now Quincy), Massachusetts, to his final days as an elder statesman. While not a complete cradle-to-grave examination, McCullough focuses on the most crucial periods of Adams' life, particularly his involvement in the American Revolution and his presidency.

The narrative begins with Adams' early years, briefly touching on his childhood and education at Harvard College. McCullough quickly moves to Adams' legal career, where he first gained prominence defending British soldiers involved in the Boston Massacre. This episode showcases Adams' commitment to the rule of law and his belief that even unpopular defendants deserved a fair trial â€“ a principle that would guide him throughout his life.

As the biography progresses, McCullough delves deep into Adams' role in the Continental Congress and his tireless efforts to push for independence from Great Britain. The author paints a vivid picture of the debates and discussions that led to the Declaration of Independence, highlighting Adams' eloquence and persuasive abilities. McCullough's depiction of Adams during this period is particularly compelling, showing a man driven by a sense of duty to his country and a firm belief in the principles of liberty and self-governance.

One of the book's strengths is its exploration of Adams' diplomatic career in Europe during and after the Revolutionary War. McCullough details Adams' efforts to secure loans and recognition for the fledgling United States, as well as his role in negotiating the Treaty of Paris, which officially ended the war. These chapters provide valuable insights into the challenges faced by the young nation on the international stage and Adams' skill as a diplomat.

The biography also shines in its portrayal of Adams' relationship with his wife, Abigail. McCullough draws extensively from the couple's correspondence, revealing a partnership of equals based on mutual respect, love, and intellectual stimulation. Abigail emerges as a formidable figure in her own right, offering counsel and support to her husband throughout his career. Their letters provide an intimate glimpse into their personal lives and the political landscape of the time.

McCullough's treatment of Adams' presidency is particularly nuanced. He presents Adams as a leader who faced significant challenges, including a quasi-war with France and intense political opposition from both Alexander Hamilton's Federalists and Thomas Jefferson's Democratic-Republicans. The author does not shy away from Adams' mistakes, such as his support for the controversial Alien and Sedition Acts, but he also highlights Adams' achievements, including his successful resolution of the conflict with France without resorting to all-out war.

One aspect of Adams' character that McCullough emphasizes is his religious fervor. The biography explores Adams' deep-rooted Puritan background and how it influenced his worldview and decision-making. McCullough presents Adams as a man of faith who saw divine providence in the success of the American Revolution and the establishment of the republic. However, the author also shows that Adams' religious beliefs were tempered by Enlightenment rationalism, leading to a complex and nuanced approach to matters of faith and governance.

The book does an excellent job of contextualizing Adams within the broader sweep of American history. McCullough draws connections between Adams and other key figures of the era, including Thomas Jefferson, Benjamin Franklin, and George Washington. The evolving relationship between Adams and Jefferson is a particular focus, with McCullough tracing their journey from close collaborators to bitter political rivals and, finally, to reconciled friends in their twilight years.

One of the most compelling aspects of McCullough's biography is his ability to humanize Adams. While celebrating his subject's achievements and virtues, the author does not shy away from Adams' flaws and shortcomings. We see Adams' vanity, his sometimes prickly personality, and his struggles with self-doubt. This balanced approach results in a more relatable and three-dimensional portrait of the man.

McCullough's prose is engaging and accessible, making the biography appealing to both scholars and general readers. His narrative style brings historical events to life, immersing the reader in the sights, sounds, and atmosphere of 18th and early 19th century America and Europe. The author's meticulous research is evident throughout the book, drawing on a wealth of primary sources, including Adams' own writings, to create a rich and detailed account of his life and times.

While the biography is comprehensive in its coverage of Adams' public life, some readers might wish for more detail on his early years and his time after leaving the presidency. McCullough's focus on Adams' political career means that certain aspects of his personal life receive less attention. However, this approach allows the author to delve deeply into the most consequential periods of Adams' life and his impact on American history.

One of the book's greatest strengths is its ability to challenge popular misconceptions about Adams. Often overshadowed by more charismatic figures like Jefferson and Washington, Adams emerges in McCullough's work as a crucial player in the founding of the United States. The biography highlights Adams' intellectual contributions to the principles of American government, his tireless work ethic, and his unwavering commitment to the nation's best interests, even when it cost him politically.

McCullough's portrayal of Adams' post-presidency years is particularly poignant. The author describes Adams' return to private life, his reconciliation with Jefferson, and his joy in seeing his son, John Quincy Adams, elected president. These later chapters provide a fitting coda to Adams' remarkable life, showing a man who, despite his setbacks and disappointments, remained proud of his contributions to his country and optimistic about its future.

The biography also offers valuable insights into the political climate of the early republic. McCullough's detailed accounts of the fierce partisanship and personal attacks that characterized the elections of 1796 and 1800 serve as a reminder that political discord is not unique to our time. Adams' navigation of these turbulent waters, often at great personal cost, underscores his commitment to the principles of republican government and the peaceful transfer of power.

One of the most thought-provoking aspects of the book is its exploration of Adams' political philosophy. McCullough presents Adams as a nuanced thinker who defied easy categorization. While often associated with the Federalists, Adams maintained an independent streak that sometimes put him at odds with his own party. His belief in a strong central government was balanced by a deep-seated fear of tyranny and a respect for individual liberties. This complexity makes Adams a fascinating subject for study, particularly in light of ongoing debates about the proper role of government in American society.

The biography also sheds light on Adams' contributions to American diplomacy and foreign policy. His experiences in Europe, both during and after the Revolution, shaped his views on international relations and influenced his approach to foreign affairs as president. McCullough's detailed account of Adams' diplomatic efforts provides valuable context for understanding the challenges faced by the young United States on the world stage.

One area where the biography particularly excels is in its depiction of Adams' personal relationships. Beyond his partnership with Abigail, McCullough explores Adams' connections with a wide range of historical figures. His complex friendship with Benjamin Franklin, his mentorship of young John Marshall (who would go on to become a legendary Chief Justice), and his sometimes fraught relationship with Alexander Hamilton all receive thoughtful treatment. These relationships help to situate Adams within the broader context of his time and illustrate the personal dynamics that often influenced political decisions.

McCullough's "John Adams" is not just a biography of one man, but a window into the birth of a nation. Through Adams' eyes, readers witness the transformation of the American colonies from subjects of the British crown to citizens of an independent republic. The book captures the excitement, uncertainty, and challenges of this pivotal period in history, making it come alive for modern readers.

In conclusion, David McCullough's "John Adams" is a monumental work that succeeds in rescuing its subject from relative obscurity and establishing him as a central figure in the founding of the United States. Through meticulous research and engaging prose, McCullough presents a nuanced and human portrait of Adams, celebrating his achievements while acknowledging his flaws. The biography offers valuable insights into the political, social, and intellectual currents of early America, making it essential reading for anyone interested in U.S. history.

For readers who may not have given much thought to John Adams before, this book serves as an excellent introduction to a fascinating and often overlooked Founding Father. McCullough's work invites us to appreciate Adams' integrity, his intellectual rigor, and his unwavering commitment to the American experiment. While it may not be a complete cradle-to-grave examination, the biography certainly captures the essence of Adams' character and his significant contributions to the nation.

Ultimately, "John Adams" is more than just a biography; it's a testament to the power of principled leadership and the enduring relevance of the ideals upon which the United States was founded. McCullough's work challenges us to consider Adams' legacy and its implications for contemporary American democracy. It reminds us that the experiment in self-governance launched by Adams and his contemporaries requires ongoing commitment and vigilance from each generation of citizens.

For those inspired by this biography to learn more about John Adams or the Founding era, McCullough's work serves as an excellent starting point for further exploration. It may well prompt readers to delve into Adams' own writings, explore other biographies of Founding Fathers, or examine the broader historical context of the American Revolution and the early republic.

In crafting this remarkable biography, David McCullough has done a great service not only to John Adams but to all students of American history. He has brought to life a pivotal figure in the nation's past, offering lessons and inspiration for the present and future. "John Adams" stands as a testament to the power of biography to illuminate not just an individual life, but an entire era.
