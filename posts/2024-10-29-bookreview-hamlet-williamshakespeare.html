Hamlet by William Shakespeare Review

October 29, 2024

Intro

In the vast canon of English literature, few works have achieved the enduring acclaim and profound complexity of William Shakespeare’s Hamlet. Written at the turn of the 17th century, this play transcends its era, delving deep into the labyrinth of human emotion and existential inquiry. At its heart lies the tragic tale of Prince Hamlet, a young man grappling with grief, vengeance, and the paralyzing weight of indecision. Shakespeare masterfully weaves together themes of mortality, madness, and moral corruption, presenting a narrative that not only reflects the tumult of its time but also resonates with contemporary audiences.

As I navigated the intricate layers of Hamlet, I found myself astounded by the depth of its characters and the philosophical dilemmas they embody. Each soliloquy peels back the veil of Hamlet’s tortured psyche, inviting readers to contemplate the nature of existence and the essence of humanity. The play's exploration of revenge and the consequences of action and inaction serves as a poignant reminder of the struggles we all face in our quest for meaning.

In this essay, I will embark on a thorough examination of Hamlet, focusing on its plot structure, thematic richness, character complexity, and the striking language that has secured its place as a monumental work of art. Through this exploration, I aim to articulate my admiration for Shakespeare’s unparalleled ability to capture the intricacies of the human experience, making Hamlet not just a play, but a timeless reflection of our shared humanity.

As the exceptional Shakespeare would say, pray heed, dear audience, for spoilers shall ensue!

Plot and Structure

Let me begin by saying I’ll be re-reading Hamlet again—perhaps even annually around Halloween. From the moment an apparition appeared on the cold, dark grounds of Elsinore Castle, I was hooked. Despite knowing little of Hamlet when I first picked it up, the eerie beginning had me from the start.

With that, I bid thee now to witness mine examination of plot and structure.

Hamlet opens in the dead of night as the ghost of King Hamlet appears to sentinels and to Horatio, Prince Hamlet’s friend. This haunting scene sets a tone of mystery and foreboding that permeates the play. As events unfold, we’re plunged into a world dense with political intrigue, family betrayal, and deep philosophical questions.

I’ll admit, I was surprised—and delighted. I had heard of Shakespeare’s extraordinary prose, but the experience was beyond what I anticipated. I found myself nostalgic for his “Old English” way of speaking, especially after reading lines like these from Act 1, Scene 2:

Hamlet: O, that this too, too solid flesh would melt, Thaw and resolve itself into a dew! How weary, stale, flat, and profitable Seem to me all the uses of this world! Fie on't! O fie! 'tis an unweeded garden That grows to seed; things rank and gross in nature Possess it merely.

This depth of expression, with such meticulous use of punctuation and pause, was astounding. I’m even tempted to incorporate this kind of language into my own speech (though I wonder if that will lead to shunning, which is the question!).

The central narrative follows Prince Hamlet’s quest for revenge upon learning from his father’s ghost that his uncle, Claudius, murdered him to seize the Danish throne. Already, the plot is brimming with tension, but paired with Shakespeare’s prose, it reaches thrilling heights.

  • Act 1 introduces the main conflict: Hamlet is grief-stricken over his father’s death and repulsed by his mother Gertrude’s swift remarriage to Claudius. When he learns of his father’s murder, his need for vengeance ignites but is accompanied by a profound existential crisis.
  • Act 2 deepens the conflict: Hamlet grapples with the moral weight of revenge. Feigning madness, he tests the loyalty of those around him, leading to manipulative encounters, particularly with Ophelia, his love, and Polonius, her father.
  • Act 3 is pivotal: Hamlet stages a reenactment of the murder to confirm Claudius’s guilt. His philosophical musings culminate in the iconic “To be, or not to be” soliloquy, where he ponders existence itself and the morality of action.
  • Act 4 sees consequences escalate: Hamlet accidentally kills Polonius, leading to Ophelia’s descent into madness and Claudius’s decision to plot Hamlet’s death. Paranoia, guilt, and grief spiral to a boiling point.
  • Act 5 culminates in tragedy: The duel between Hamlet and Laertes sparks a tragic domino effect, resulting in the deaths of nearly every main character, including Hamlet himself. Fortinbras’s arrival at the end signals the restoration of order.

The structure of Hamlet amplifies dramatic tension while also revealing Hamlet’s multifaceted character. Shakespeare’s use of soliloquies throughout allows for an intimate look into Hamlet’s inner struggles, giving us insight into his moral and existential dilemmas.

For me, these inner monologues enrich the story in a way few plays manage. It’s something I crave in fiction—the deeply personal reflections that make a character feel real. Here, Hamlet sets the bar high, and this reading experience has left a mark.

Through this intricate plot and structure, Hamlet offers a profound meditation on revenge, mortality, and the human condition, securing its place as a timeless work that speaks across the ages.
