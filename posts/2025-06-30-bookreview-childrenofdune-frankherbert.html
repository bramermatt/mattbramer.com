Children of Dune by Frank Herbert Book Review

About the links: Links in this review point to publishers, retailers, author sites, or Wikipedia for more info. All open in a new tab so you won’t lose your place.

Started Reading 2025.06.19. Finished Reading 2025.06.30.

Oh man. Children of Dune absolutely blew me away.

Plot

This book is tight. It’s everything I love about Dune — the mysticism, the politics, the harsh beauty of Arrakis — but turned up a notch. It's like the desert winds blew the veil off the future, and Frank Herbert walked us straight into something ancient, prophetic, and terrifyingly beautiful.

The story picks up after Dune Messiah, with Paul gone, Muad'Dib is now legend, and his children — Leto II and Ghanima — at the center of a whirlwind of prophecy, power, and fear. But what makes Children of Dune so awesome isn't just the plot (which is killer) — it’s the secrets. We go places. Secret places. Hidden tunnels beneath Sietches. Hidden layers of consciousness. Hidden motives wrapped in religious fervor and political ambition. It’s secrets on secrets — and every time one’s revealed, it leads to something even deeper. I mean, this is incredible. My brain breaks and I love it. The combination of words that describe tri-ocular view that opens the veils of time... ahhh, so good.

The Golden Path

The book grapples with destiny in such a unique way, and really the entire Dune series does. Leto II begins to glimpse what’s called the Golden Path, and without spoiling too much — wow. It's heavy, it’s philosophical, and it’s epic in the truest sense. He’s not just making decisions for the present; he’s making choices that will shape humanity for thousands of years. And yet it never feels too abstract, again, it's awesome. It's haunting. Awesome.

The World

Arrakis just keeps getting better and better in these books. It’s not just a desert anymore — it feels alive. You can sense the changes happening, the tension between the old ways and the new. Fremen who once survived on spice and ritual now talk about planting trees and changing the land. And you can tell not everyone’s on board. The land itself almost feels like it’s resisting.

I loved all the small details — the worms, the sietch tunnels, the smell of spice in the air. Herbert doesn’t just describe a planet; he builds a whole spiritual ecosystem. It’s beautiful, dangerous, and sacred. You feel like if you took one step in the wrong direction, the planet might swallow you whole. It makes everything feel bigger, like the stakes aren’t just political or personal — they’re planetary.

Pre-Born

The whole “pre-born” concept is wild. Leto and Ghanima aren’t just kids — they’ve got all the memories of their ancestors. Literally thousands of lives in their heads. That alone is nuts, but the way they live with it is what got me. Every conversation, every decision they make — it’s like they’re filtering it through the wisdom (and trauma) of generations. And yet they’re still trying to be themselves.

There’s this tension between holding onto who they are and not getting lost in the crowd inside their minds. Alia failed that test. Leto and Ghanima are constantly on the edge of losing themselves too. That makes even the small moments feel intense. I kept thinking how lonely that must be — to never truly have a thought that’s just your own. And yet, they carry it like it’s normal. It’s incredible.

Jessica Atreides

I really liked Jessica in this one. She’s not just back to be the wise mom — she’s sharp. Suspicious. Strategic. And at the same time, she cares about Leto and Ghanima in a way that feels genuine. You can tell she’s still carrying the weight of everything that happened with Paul, with the Bene Gesserit, and now she’s got to walk this tightrope between loyalty to her order and love for her family.

What I appreciated most is how Jessica sees through Alia right away. She knows something’s off. She doesn’t play games — she moves carefully, but she’s always ten steps ahead. It reminded me that in the Dune universe, the older generation still has a big part to play. Jessica’s presence adds this quiet pressure to the whole story, like she’s the last person who really remembers what it all cost.

Alia

And Alia... her story hit me hard. Watching her unravel was like watching a fire slowly consume something beautiful. She’s torn apart from the inside by the memories and the Baron’s voice — it’s terrifying and tragic. She knows what’s happening to her, and that makes it worse. Her descent into Abomination stands in direct contrast to Leto’s rise, and it makes the stakes so much more real. It’s one of the saddest arcs in the book, and it stayed with me.

Leto II

This book also has one of the most chilling and fascinating transformations I’ve ever read. Leto II… man, what a character. His journey is both monstrous and messianic. By the end, I was floored. What he becomes. What he embraces. It’s not just sci-fi — it’s mythic. AWESOME. I have heard about what happens to Leto II in this series, and that's what I'm racing towards, and we are getting "a beginning"... I love it!

Every character feels like they're standing on the edge of something greater than themselves, and watching them wrestle with that tension is just... awesome.

Prose

And can we talk about the writing? Herbert’s prose is dense, sure, but once you're locked in, it's hypnotic. That's the best word for it. I'm a little entranced as if I've taken on the melange! There's a rhythm to the way he reveals thoughts, layers of prescience, inner conflict, and theological depth.

Final Thoughts

In short: Children of Dune is incredible. If Dune planted the seeds, Children of Dune is the desert bloom that grows out of it: strange, fierce, and unforgettable. This is one of those books I’ll come back to again and again. I loved it. I love the Dune series. And with this one, I can feel the future opening wide — strange, golden, and full of purpose.
