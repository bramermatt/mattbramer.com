Doing Justice: A Prosecutor's Thoughts on Crime, Punishment, and the Rule of Law Book Review

Doing Justice: A Prosecutor's Thoughts on Crime, Punishment, and the Rule of Law by Preet Bharara

My rating: 5 of 5 stars

Preet Bharara’s Doing Justice is a really engaging and eye-opening look at crime, punishment, and what it actually means to pursue justice. What stood out to me most is how human the whole system is. We often think of the law as something rigid and objective, but Bharara makes it clear that it depends heavily on the judgment, character, and restraint of the people enforcing it.

Coming into this book, most of my understanding of the legal system came from reading things like The Federalist Papers, Nine Black Robes, and a handful of presidential biographies. Those works helped me see the structure and philosophy behind the system, but Doing Justice brings it down to the ground level. It shows what justice looks like in real life—messy, complicated, and often filled with difficult decisions that don’t have perfect answers.

One of the most helpful parts of the book is how Bharara organizes it around a set of principles like fairness, consistency, and humility. He doesn’t just define these ideas—he shows how they actually play out in real cases. That makes the book feel practical instead of theoretical. It also highlights something I hadn’t thought much about before: sometimes justice isn’t about what you can do, but what you should do. The idea of restraint—of choosing not to act even when you have the power to—is a big theme, and it really stuck with me.

His discussion of prosecutorial (prosecutor) discretion was especially interesting. Laws might be written clearly, but applying them is anything but automatic. The decisions prosecutors make can shape lives in major ways, and those decisions require wisdom, not just knowledge. That ties back to something you see hinted at in older writings like The Federalist Papers—the system only works well when the people inside it are people of integrity.

Bharara also does a great job telling stories. He walks through different cases that show the kinds of moral and ethical challenges prosecutors face. These aren’t over-the-top or dramatic for the sake of entertainment—they’re thoughtful and help you see just how complex justice can be. Very rarely is anything completely black and white.

Another thing I appreciated is how readable the book is. Bharara doesn’t get lost in legal jargon or try to sound overly academic. He writes clearly and directly, which makes the book approachable even if you don’t have a legal background. It feels like you’re learning from someone who knows the system well but wants you to understand it too.

Overall, Doing Justice is more than just a book about law. It’s really about responsibility—what it means to make fair decisions, to hold power carefully, and to pursue justice with integrity. For me, it filled in a gap between the theory I’ve read and the reality of how things actually work. It’s a thoughtful, accessible, and genuinely enlightening read.
