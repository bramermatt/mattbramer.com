The Wrong Stuff: How the Soviet Space Program Crashed and Burned Book Review

The Wrong Stuff: How the Soviet Space Program Crashed and Burned by John Strausbaugh

My rating: 3 of 5 stars

Review of The Wrong Stuff by John Strausbaugh

I picked up The Wrong Stuff without having read The Right Stuff, so I wasn’t coming in with a built-in comparison—just a general interest in space, flight, and how all of this actually worked (or didn’t). And honestly, that made this book a pretty engaging entry point.

Strausbaugh tells the story of the Soviet space program in a way that feels almost unbelievable at times—like a mix of ingenuity and chaos. You get stories of rushed launches, risky decisions, and missions that seem one step away from disaster. It’s the kind of book where you keep stopping and thinking, “There’s no way they actually did that… right?” And yet, they did—and somehow still managed to rack up major “firsts” in space.

The writing is probably the biggest strength. It’s quick, sarcastic, and very readable. It doesn’t feel like a textbook at all—it feels like someone telling you wild stories from history, and that makes it easy to keep turning pages.

But the more I read, the more I started to feel the limitation: it leans too hard in one direction. The Soviets come across as reckless, sloppy, and almost lucky more than anything else. And while there’s definitely truth to the danger and pressure they operated under, it starts to feel like the book is flattening the story instead of deepening it.

That’s not just my reaction either. In a review by Jeff Foust for The Space Review, he argues the book “appears to be to summarize and synthesize what others have written” and even “intended to sensationalize” the story rather than add real depth . You can read that full review here: Read the review on The Space Review￼

And I think that nails it. The book is entertaining—but it sometimes trades understanding for storytelling. You walk away with a strong impression of chaos, but maybe not a full appreciation of the real engineering, intelligence, and determination that made those achievements possible in the first place.

So overall, I’d say this: The Wrong Stuff is a fun, engaging read that got me more interested in space history—but it’s not the whole picture. It’s probably best read as a starting point, not the final word.
