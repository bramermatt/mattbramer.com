Fancy Bear Goes Phishing: The Dark History of the Information Age, in Five Extraordinary Hacks Book Review

Fancy Bear Goes Phishing: The Dark History of the Information Age, in Five Extraordinary Hacks by Scott J. Shapiro

My rating: 3 of 5 stars

Fancy Bear Goes Phishing is a fascinating and deeply philosophical exploration of hacking, cybersecurity, and the nature of the internet itself. What stood out most to me was how much the book treats hacking not just as a technical issue, but as something tied to ethics, systems, communication, and human behavior.

At the same time, that philosophical angle was probably my biggest challenge with the book. Scott Shapiro clearly knows his material and is an excellent thinker, but the writing sometimes leaned heavily into philosophical language and abstract ideas that pulled me out of the reading experience. My own learning and communication style is much more direct and visual, so some of the more esoteric discussions were harder for me to stay engaged with.

Still, I genuinely enjoyed the book. The sections on actual hacks, internet infrastructure, and the science behind computing were incredibly engaging. As someone with a computer science background who’s currently focused more on physics, chemistry, and math, it was refreshing to dive back into the world of computers and networks. The book reminded me how fascinating computer science really is and honestly made me want to sit back down at a terminal and start programming again.

The book does move into political territory at times — particularly around Donald Trump and modern cyberwarfare — though that almost feels unavoidable given the subject matter. Overall, even when I struggled with the writing style, I found the ideas compelling and the subject matter consistently interesting.

This isn’t a light or casual cybersecurity book. It’s thoughtful, intellectual, and sometimes dense, but if you’re interested in the deeper implications of hacking and the internet, it’s absolutely worth reading.
