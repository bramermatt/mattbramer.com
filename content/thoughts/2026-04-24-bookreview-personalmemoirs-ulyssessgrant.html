Personal Memoirs Book Review

Personal Memoirs by Ulysses S. Grant

My rating: 5 of 5 stars

I read The Personal Memoirs of Ulysses S. Grant after finishing Ron Chernow’s Grant last year, and I still found myself wanting more—more of Grant himself, not just the story of his life. This book absolutely delivers on that.

What surprised me most is just how real it feels. Grant doesn’t come across like a distant historical figure trying to shape how he’ll be remembered. Instead, he writes like someone sitting across the table from you, just telling you what happened. There’s a straightforwardness to his voice that I haven’t experienced in many books, especially not from someone of his stature. It almost feels conversational, even when he’s describing major battles or decisions that shaped the course of the country.

That simplicity is part of what makes the memoir so powerful. He explains military movements, strategies, and challenges in a way that’s clear without being oversimplified. You don’t need to be a Civil War expert to follow along, but you also don’t feel like anything is watered down. There’s a quiet confidence in how he writes—he doesn’t need to exaggerate anything, because the events themselves carry enough weight.

Another thing that stood out to me is his tone. Grant is remarkably humble. He gives credit to others, takes responsibility when needed, and avoids the kind of self-promotion you might expect from someone in his position. Even when he’s describing victories, there’s a sense of restraint that makes you trust him more, not less.

I will say, I was a little surprised (and maybe caught off guard) that there’s basically nothing about his presidency. At first, I wondered if something was missing, but I realized that’s really on me—I didn’t fully think through what a “memoir” is supposed to be. Grant clearly chose to focus on what mattered most to him: his military experiences and the events surrounding the war. And honestly, that focus works. It keeps the book tight and purposeful.

Reading this made me appreciate Grant even more—not just as a general, but as a communicator. His writing reflects the same qualities you’d want in a leader: clarity, steadiness, and honesty. There’s no unnecessary flourish, just a consistent effort to tell things as they happened.

If you’ve read Chernow’s biography (even if it’s been a while like it has for me) and you’re still curious about Grant, this is the perfect next step. It doesn’t just add information—it gives you Grant himself. And that’s what makes it such an excellent read.
