Failure is Not an Option: Mission Control From Mercury to Apollo 13 and Beyond Book Review

Failure is Not an Option: Mission Control From Mercury to Apollo 13 and Beyond by Gene Kranz

My rating: 5 of 5 stars

Few books manage to teach history, engineering, leadership, and human courage all at once, but Failure Is Not an Option by Gene Kranz accomplishes exactly that. More than just a memoir, this book is a front-row seat to the birth of America’s space program and the intense pressure-filled world of Mission Control during the Space Race. It is easily one of the best books available for anyone who wants to understand NASA, Apollo, and the spirit of America during the 1960s and 1970s.

What makes this book so powerful is that it is not written like a distant historical summary. Kranz was there. He lived it. He was one of the legendary flight directors who guided missions from Mercury through Apollo, including the near-disaster of Apollo 13. Every chapter feels personal because it comes from someone who experienced the tension, excitement, failures, and triumphs firsthand. You are not simply reading about history — you feel immersed in it.

One of the greatest strengths of the book is how vividly it captures the atmosphere of early NASA. Kranz describes the long hours, impossible deadlines, and relentless determination that defined the engineers and controllers who worked in Mission Control. Many of them were young, inexperienced, and learning as they went, yet they carried the enormous responsibility of sending astronauts into space and bringing them safely home. The book constantly reminds the reader that space exploration was not inevitable. It was accomplished by ordinary people willing to pursue extraordinary goals.

The sections covering the Mercury and Gemini programs are fascinating because they reveal how much trial and error occurred before Apollo ever reached the Moon. The reader sees NASA grow from a fragile organization still figuring out basic procedures into the disciplined machine capable of landing men on the lunar surface. Kranz explains complex technical situations clearly enough for non-engineers to follow while still respecting the intelligence of the reader. Even someone with little scientific background can appreciate the challenges involved.

Of course, the emotional centerpiece of the book is Apollo 13. Kranz’s account of the explosion and the desperate effort to save the crew is absolutely gripping, even if you already know the outcome. The famous phrase “failure is not an option” perfectly captures the mindset of Mission Control. The book shows how teamwork, preparation, discipline, and calm leadership allowed NASA to overcome what seemed impossible. These chapters are some of the most intense and inspiring pages I have ever read.

What gives the book even more weight is its ending. Kranz reflects on the state of America’s space program after Apollo and speaks honestly about how politics became deeply intertwined with NASA’s future. During the height of the Space Race, there was a national urgency and unity that pushed innovation forward at an astonishing pace. But after the Moon landings, shifting political priorities, budget battles, and changing public interest slowed the momentum that once carried America to the lunar surface. Kranz writes with both pride and disappointment — pride in what NASA accomplished, but disappointment that the bold spirit of exploration often became dependent on political cycles and short-term thinking. His reflections make the reader wonder what could have been achieved if the same determination of the Apollo era had continued uninterrupted.

Ultimately, *Failure Is Not an Option* succeeds because it combines historical significance with genuine humanity. Gene Kranz writes with humility, honesty, and passion, making the reader care not only about the missions but also about the people behind them. Whether you are interested in NASA, engineering, American history, leadership, or simply great storytelling, this book is essential reading. It does not just explain the Space Race — it makes you feel what it was like to live through it, and it leaves you thinking about where the future of exploration may go next.
