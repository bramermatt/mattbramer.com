Project Hail Mary Book Review

Project Hail Mary by Andy Weir

My rating: 3 of 5 stars

Project Hail Mary — Book Review (3/5)

I finally finished Project Hail Mary by Andy Weir, and overall I enjoyed it quite a bit. It was a fun read, an easy read, and one that kept me turning pages. The science was fascinating, the central friendship was excellent, and the story had enough mystery and momentum to keep me invested. That said, I don't think I loved it quite as much as many readers seem to. For me, this lands somewhere around a 3/5.

One of the biggest strengths of the novel is the science. Weir has a gift for taking complicated scientific ideas and making them feel approachable, exciting, and relevant to the story. I won't pretend I followed every calculation or scientific explanation perfectly, but I followed enough to appreciate what he was doing. The book constantly presents a problem and then invites the reader to think through possible solutions alongside the characters. That's part of what makes it so engaging.

What I especially appreciated was how grounded much of the science felt. Even when the story ventured into speculative territory, it was built upon real-world principles and ideas. It felt like science fiction that respected science rather than simply using scientific words as decoration. The result was a book that made me curious and interested rather than confused or overwhelmed.

The characters are a little more of a mixed bag.

Ryland Grace and Rocky are absolutely the stars of the show. Their relationship is easily the strongest part of the novel. Watching two intelligent beings from entirely different worlds learn to communicate, cooperate, and eventually become genuine friends was wonderful. Rocky is one of the most memorable science fiction characters I've encountered in a long time. His personality shines through despite the enormous differences between him and Grace, and the friendship between the two feels earned.

Grace also grew on me over the course of the novel. Early on, I was worried that I wasn't going to connect with him. He initially felt like the standard Andy Weir protagonist: extremely smart, constantly sarcastic, and always ready with a joke. Sometimes that style works for me, and sometimes it feels a little forced. In the opening chapters, I found myself wondering if I was just getting another version of the same character Weir has written before.

Thankfully, Grace develops beyond that. As the story unfolds and his memories return, he becomes more nuanced and sympathetic. By the end, I genuinely cared about him and was invested in his choices.

The rest of the cast, however, felt fairly thin. Most of the supporting characters exist primarily to serve the plot rather than to feel like fully realized people. Stratt is probably the most memorable among them, but even she functions more as a force of nature than as a deeply developed character. I don't necessarily think this hurts the book, since so much of the story depends on Grace's isolation, but it does create a noticeable contrast between how fleshed out Grace and Rocky feel compared to almost everyone else.

I also have mixed feelings about the amnesia structure.

On one hand, it works. It creates mystery, allows the reader to discover information alongside Grace, and provides a natural way to reveal the backstory without resorting to giant exposition dumps. On the other hand, the mechanism itself sometimes felt a little clunky. There were moments where the process seemed to boil down to Grace thinking really hard and suddenly unlocking another memory. It was effective enough, but I occasionally became aware of the author's hand moving pieces into place.

My biggest criticism, though, is the ending.

The final stretch felt surprisingly rushed compared to the rest of the novel. The book spends hundreds of pages carefully building problems, solving problems, introducing new complications, and exploring the science behind everything. Then suddenly it felt like the story hit lightspeed and raced toward the finish line.

I don't dislike where the story ends. In fact, I think Grace's ultimate decision is completely fitting for his character arc. Thematically, it makes sense. Emotionally, it makes sense. I even like the image of where we leave him.

What felt lacking was the journey to get there.

Throughout the book, there were several moments where it seemed like things simply had to go wrong because the story needed another obstacle. Every time progress was made, another disaster appeared around the corner. That's not necessarily bad storytelling—conflict is essential—but eventually it started to feel like the novel was piling on problems faster than it was creating satisfying resolutions.

By the time the ending arrived, I was expecting a little more room to breathe. Instead, it felt like the book rapidly tied off its remaining threads and came to a close. I wanted more reflection. More aftermath. More exploration of the consequences of everything that had happened. The ending wasn't bad—it just felt somewhat anti-climactic given the scale of the journey.

Even with those criticisms, I had a good time reading Project Hail Mary. The science is entertaining, the pacing is generally strong, and the friendship between Grace and Rocky carries the book. There are moments of genuine wonder, clever problem-solving, and heartfelt emotion that remind me why science fiction can be such a rewarding genre.

It's not a perfect novel. Some of the character work outside the main duo is thin. The amnesia device occasionally shows its seams. The ending moves faster than I would have liked. But it remains an enjoyable adventure filled with interesting ideas and memorable moments.

Most importantly, it was fun.

And sometimes that's enough.

Rating: 3/5

A fun, science-heavy adventure with a fantastic central friendship, fascinating ideas, and an ending that left me wishing it had spent a little more time earning its conclusion. I'm looking forward to seeing how the movie adapts it.
