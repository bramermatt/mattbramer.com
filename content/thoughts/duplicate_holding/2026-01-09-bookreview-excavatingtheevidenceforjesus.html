Excavating the Evidence for Jesus: The Archaeology and History of Christ and the Gospels Book Review

Excavating the Evidence for Jesus by Dr. Titus M. Kennedy was an incredibly enjoyable and encouraging read. As someone who has become increasingly interested in biblical archaeology and the historical foundations of Scripture, this book felt like a natural next step. Kennedy does not write to overwhelm the reader with technical details or to win an argument, but to invite the reader into the historical world of Jesus and show how evidence—both archaeological and textual—supports the reality of His life and impact.

One of the things I appreciated most about this book is how clearly and calmly Kennedy approaches the subject. In a time when discussions about Jesus often become polarized, sensationalized, or dismissive, Kennedy takes a steady and thoughtful approach. He does not claim that archaeology can “prove” every aspect of Christianity, nor does he pretend that faith depends entirely on physical evidence. Instead, he shows how history and archaeology consistently point to Jesus as a real person who lived, taught, and was crucified in first-century Judea.

Early in the book, Kennedy addresses the idea—popular in some circles—that Jesus may not have existed at all. Rather than reacting defensively, he patiently explains why this view is rejected by nearly all professional historians. He walks through ancient non-Christian sources such as Roman and Jewish writers who mention Jesus and the early Christian movement. What stood out to me here was how restrained Kennedy is. He does not exaggerate what these sources say, but he makes it clear that they firmly establish Jesus as a historical figure. That honesty made his conclusions far more convincing.

Kennedy then moves into how archaeology fits into the conversation. He is very clear that archaeology rarely gives us direct evidence of individuals like Jesus—no personal artifacts or inscriptions with His name—but that is not how archaeology normally works. Instead, what archaeology does exceptionally well is confirm the setting of the Gospels. As Kennedy explains, the places, customs, political figures, and daily life described in the New Testament align remarkably well with what archaeologists have uncovered.

Some of the most interesting sections of the book focus on specific locations and practices mentioned in the Gospels. Sites such as the Pool of Bethesda, the Pool of Siloam, Nazareth, and Capernaum help show that the Gospel writers were deeply familiar with the geography and culture of their time. Kennedy also discusses crucifixion and burial practices, which add important historical context to the accounts of Jesus’ death. Reading these sections helped me better visualize the world of the New Testament and made the Gospel narratives feel even more grounded in real history.

Another strength of this book is how Kennedy handles the topic of the resurrection. He does not attempt to “prove” the resurrection through archaeology, and he is upfront about that. Instead, he focuses on what can be established historically: that Jesus was crucified, that His followers genuinely believed He rose from the dead, and that this belief transformed a small group of fearful disciples into a bold and growing movement. Kennedy shows that the resurrection belief appeared very early and was not a later legend. I appreciated this careful distinction between historical evidence and faith, which allowed the discussion to remain honest and credible.

Kennedy also takes time to address common claims found in documentaries, internet articles, and popular books that suggest hidden evidence, secret gospels, or pagan myths behind Christianity. Rather than mocking these ideas, he explains where they fall apart historically. This was especially helpful, as it equipped me with a clearer understanding of how scholars evaluate sources and why many popular claims simply do not hold up under serious examination.

Throughout the book, Kennedy’s tone remains respectful and inviting. He writes in a way that is easy to follow, even for readers without a background in archaeology or ancient history. At the same time, it is clear that his conclusions are grounded in serious scholarship. This balance makes the book both approachable and trustworthy.

On a personal level, Excavating the Evidence for Jesus deepened my appreciation for the historical foundations of my faith. Rather than replacing faith with evidence, the book showed how evidence can support and strengthen belief. It also sparked a desire to continue learning. Like Unearthing the Bible, this book did not close the door on the subject—it opened it wider. I found myself wanting to read more, study more, and better understand the historical world in which the Bible was written.

In conclusion, Excavating the Evidence for Jesus is a thoughtful, engaging, and encouraging book. Dr. Titus M. Kennedy does an excellent job showing that belief in Jesus is not based on myth or blind tradition, but on events rooted in real history. This book is an excellent resource for anyone curious about the historical Jesus, interested in biblical archaeology, or looking for a clearer understanding of how faith and history intersect. I would highly recommend it, especially for readers who want solid information presented in a clear and conversational way.
